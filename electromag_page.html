Module Topic 1: Maxwell's Equations Foundation

The Four Maxwell Equations

Underpins Everything

These four equations govern all classical electromagnetic behaviour. In differential (point) form:

∇ · D = ρ⁯ Gauss's Law (electric) — charges create E-fields ∇ · B = 0 Gauss's Law (magnetic) — no magnetic monopoles ∇ × E = −∂B/∂t Faraday's Law — changing B induces E ∇ × H = J + ∂D/∂t Ampere's Law — currents + changing E create H

Constitutive Relations — link D, B, J to E and H via material properties:

D = εE = ε₀εᵣE electric displacement field B = μH = μ₀μᵣH magnetic flux density J = σE Ohm's Law (conduction current density) Constants: ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻&sup7; H/m c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀) = 3 × 10&sup8; m/s
For a simple lossless non-magnetic medium: σ = 0, μᵣ = 1. Ampere's Law becomes ∇×H = ε∂E/∂t only.

Integral form (useful for symmetric problems):

∮ D · dS = Qₛₙₜ total free charge enclosed ∮ B · dS = 0 ∮ E · dl = −d/dt ∫ B · dS induced EMF ∮ H · dl = Iₛₙₜ + d/dt ∫ D · dS
The ∂D/∂t term (displacement current) was Maxwell's key insight — it completes the equations and makes EM wave propagation in free space possible.

Wave Equation from Maxwell's Equations

Key Derivation

Take the curl of Faraday's Law and substitute Ampere's Law:

∇²E − με ∂²E/∂t² = 0 vector wave equation for E ∇²H − με ∂²H/∂t² = 0 vector wave equation for H Phase velocity in medium: v = 1/√(με) = c/√(εᵣμᵣ) = c/n Refractive index: n = √(εᵣμᵣ) ≈ √εᵣ (for non-magnetic media)
You don't need to reproduce the full derivation in exam — but know the result and where it comes from.

Module Topic 2: Propagation in Lossless Media Q1a–d · Q2 · Q3a

Plane Wave Fundamentals

Q1d, Q3a
E(r,t) = E₀ cos(ωt − k·r) êₐ H(r,t) = H₀ cos(ωt − k·r) êₒ Key relations (lossless medium): ω = 2πf angular frequency [rad/s] k = ω√(με) = ω/v = 2π/λ wavenumber [rad/m] λ = v/f = 2π/k wavelength [m] v = c/n = c/√εᵣ phase velocity [m/s] Wave impedance: η = E₀/H₀ = √(μ/ε) = η₀/n η₀ = 377 Ω ≈ 120π Ω free space impedance

TEM wave orthogonality: E, H, and k̂ form a right-handed set:

k̂ · E = 0 E is perpendicular to direction of travel k̂ · H = 0 H is perpendicular to direction of travel H = (k̂ × E) / η H is found from E and propagation direction
For Q3a with k = 7êx + 5êy: choose E = E₀êz (perpendicular to k). Then H = (k̂ × E₀êz)/η₀. Compute |k| = √(49+25) = √74, so k̂ = k/|k|.

Poynting Vector — Power Flow

Q3a iv
Instantaneous: S = E × H [W/m²] Time-averaged (use for sinusoidal fields): ⟨S⟩ = ½ Re(E × H*) [W/m²] For a real plane wave: ⟨S⟩ = E₀²/(2η) k̂ = ½ E₀H₀ k̂ For E₀ = 1 V/m in free space: |⟨S⟩| = 1/(2×377) ≈ 1.33 × 10⁻³ W/m²
Direction of ⟨S⟩ always equals k̂ — the direction energy flows. Magnitude is intensity (power per unit area).

Coulomb's Law — Electric Field from Point Charges

Q1a, Q1b
E(r) = (q / 4πε₀) × (r − r') / |r − r'|³ r = field point (where you want E) r' = source point (location of charge q) R = r − r' (separation vector, always FROM charge TO field point) R̂ = R/|R| Superposition for N charges: E = Σᵢ (qᵢ / 4πε₀) × (r − rᵢ) / |r − rᵢ|³
  1. Convert all positions to Cartesian (from cylindrical/spherical if needed)
  2. Compute R = r − r', then |R|, then R̂ = R/|R|
  3. Plug into Coulomb formula
  4. For multiple charges: sum each E vector component-by-component
This paper mixes cylindrical (ρ, φ, z) and Cartesian notation. Convert to Cartesian first: êρ at angle φ = cosφ êx + sinφ êy.

Electric Potential & Voltage

Q1c, Q2b
Potential difference: Vₛₘ = V(rₛ) − V(rₘ) evaluate V at each point and subtract For point charge at origin: V(r) = Q / (4πεr) where r = |r| E from potential (gradient): E = −∇V = −(∂V/∂x êx + ∂V/∂y êy + ∂V/∂z êz) Line integral method: Vₛₘ = −∫ₘ→ₛ E · dl
For Q2b: taking the gradient of V = Q/(4πεr) in spherical coords gives E = Q/(4πεr²) êr — Coulomb's law recovered. Use as a sanity check.

Surface Charge Integration

Q2a
dE = (ρs dS / 4πε₀) × (r − r') / |r − r'|³ E = ∫∫ dE For flat plate in xy-plane: dS = dx' dy' r' = x'êx + y'êy (source on plate) r = zêz (field point above centre) Infinite sheet approximation (valid when h « plate dimensions): E ≈ ρs/(2ε₀) ên For Q2a: ρs = 2 μC/m², h = 1 mm « 10 cm plate E ≈ (2×10⁻&sup6;) / (2×8.854×10⁻¹²) ≈ 113 kV/m
Q2a iii says to derive the integral — NOT evaluate it. Set up the integrand, differential area dS, and limits correctly. Full marks for setup alone.

Module Topic 3: Propagation in Lossy Media Likely Exam Topic — Not in Sample

Complex Propagation Constant & Attenuation

Core Theory

When a medium has conductivity σ ≠ 0, the wave decays as it propagates. The propagation constant is now complex:

γ = α + jβ α = attenuation constant [Np/m] — controls amplitude decay β = phase constant [rad/m] — controls phase variation General expressions: α = ω√(με/2) × [√(1+(σ/ωε)²) − 1]^(1/2) β = ω√(με/2) × [√(1+(σ/ωε)²) + 1]^(1/2) Wave in lossy medium (propagating +z): E(z,t) = E₀ e^(−αz) cos(ωt − βz) êx decays exponentially
After distance δ = 1/α (skin depth), the field amplitude drops to 1/e ≈ 37% of its value. Power drops to 1/e² ≈ 14%.

Loss Tangent, Skin Depth & Approximations

Key Parameters
Loss tangent: tan δ = σ/(ωε) conduction current / displacement current Classification: tan δ « 1 → good dielectric (low loss) — e.g. glass, PTFE tan δ » 1 → good conductor (high loss) — e.g. copper, sea water Good conductor (σ » ωε): α ≈ β ≈ √(πfμσ) = √(ωμσ/2) Skin depth: δs = 1/α = √(2/(ωμσ)) = √(2/(2πfμσ)) Complex wave impedance in good conductor: ηc ≈ (1+j)/(σδs) Good dielectric (σ « ωε): α ≈ (σ/2)√(μ/ε) small attenuation β ≈ ω√(με) same as lossless case
Skin depth explains why high-frequency current only flows at the surface of conductors. At 1 GHz in copper: δs ≈ 2 μm — the current is in a 2 μm surface layer.

Power Absorption in Lossy Media

Links to EMC/Safety
Time-averaged Poynting vector in lossy medium: ⟨S⟩ = ½ Re(E × H*) = ½ |E₀|²/|ηc| × e^(−2αz) k̂ Note: power decays as e^(−2αz) (field decays as e^(−αz)) Ohmic power dissipated per unit volume: ⟨Pv⟩ = ½ σ|E|² [W/m³]
In medical applications (MRI, microwave hyperthermia), this power absorption heats tissue. This is the physical basis for the SAR metric in Topic 7.

Module Topic 4: Waves at Boundaries Q1e — Direct Exam Question

Normal Incidence

Simple Case First
Reflection coefficient: Γ = (η&sub2; − η&sub1;) / (η&sub2; + η&sub1;) Transmission coefficient: τ = 2η&sub2; / (η&sub2; + η&sub1;) Relation: τ = 1 + Γ Power check: |Γ|² + (η&sub1;/η&sub2;)|τ|² = 1 Example (free space → εr = 4): η&sub1; = 377 Ω, η&sub2; = 377/2 = 188.5 Ω Γ = (188.5−377)/(188.5+377) = −0.333 τ = 0.667

Oblique Incidence — Snell's Law & Fresnel Coefficients

Q1e
Snell's Law: n&sub1; sinθᵢ = n&sub2; sinθₜ n = √(εr) for non-magnetic media Angle of reflection = angle of incidence: θr = θᵢ For Q1e: n&sub1; = 1 (free space), εr = 16 → n&sub2; = 4, θᵢ = 15° sinθₜ = (1/4)×sin15° = 0.0647 → θₜ ≈ 3.71°

TE (s-pol) — E ⊥ plane of incidence:

Γ_TE = (η&sub2;cosθᵢ − η&sub1;cosθₜ) / (η&sub2;cosθᵢ + η&sub1;cosθₜ) τ_TE = 2η&sub2;cosθᵢ / (η&sub2;cosθᵢ + η&sub1;cosθₜ) Check: τ = 1 + Γ (TE only)

TM (p-pol) — H ⊥ plane of incidence:

Γ_TM = (η&sub2;cosθₜ − η&sub1;cosθᵢ) / (η&sub2;cosθₜ + η&sub1;cosθᵢ) τ_TM = 2η&sub2;cosθᵢ / (η&sub2;cosθₜ + η&sub1;cosθᵢ) Brewster angle: tanθB = n&sub2;/n&sub1;
Worked answer for Q1e: η&sub1; = 377 Ω, η&sub2; = 377/4 = 94.25 Ω cos15° = 0.9659, cos3.71° = 0.9979 Γ_TE = (94.25×0.9659 − 377×0.9979) / (94.25×0.9659 + 377×0.9979) ≈ (91.0 − 376.3) / (91.0 + 376.3) = −0.611 τ_TE = 1 + Γ ≈ 0.389
Q1e says "transverse electric wave" = TE polarisation. Use the TE Fresnel formulas, not TM.

Total Internal Reflection (dense → less dense medium):

Critical angle: sinθc = n&sub2;/n&sub1; (requires n&sub1; > n&sub2;) For θᵢ > θc: |Γ| = 1, no transmitted wave (Used in optical fibres — light stays trapped inside)

Boundary Conditions

Maxwell Derived
At any interface between two media: E&sub1;t = E&sub2;t tangential E continuous H&sub1;t − H&sub2;t = Js = 0 for dielectric interface D&sub1;n − D&sub2;n = ρs = 0 if no surface charge B&sub1;n = B&sub2;n normal B always continuous
These boundary conditions follow directly from Maxwell's equations in integral form. They are the starting point for deriving Snell's law and Fresnel coefficients.

Module Topic 5: Transmission Lines May Appear — Not in Sample

Transmission Line Model & Equations

Core Theory

A transmission line (coax cable, PCB trace, power line) is modelled by distributed R, L, G, C per unit length:

Telegrapher's equations: ∂V/∂z = −(R + jωL)I ∂I/∂z = −(G + jωC)V Propagation constant: γ = √((R+jωL)(G+jωC)) = α + jβ Characteristic impedance: Z₀ = √((R+jωL)/(G+jωC)) Lossless line (R = G = 0): γ = jβ = jω√(LC) Z₀ = √(L/C) real, purely geometry-dependent v = 1/√(LC) = c/√(εr) phase velocity on line
For coaxial cable: Z₀ = (η/2π)ln(b/a) where b, a are outer and inner conductor radii. 50 Ω is standard RF. 75 Ω is standard for TV/video.

Reflections, VSWR & Impedance Matching

Key Applications
Reflection coefficient at load Zⁿ: Γⁿ = (Zⁿ − Z₀) / (Zⁿ + Z₀) Special cases: Zⁿ = Z₀ (matched) → Γ = 0 no reflection Zⁿ = 0 (short) → Γ = −1 total reflection, voltage zero at load Zⁿ = ∞ (open) → Γ = +1 total reflection, current zero at load VSWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 − |Γ|) VSWR = 1: perfect match; VSWR = ∞: total reflection Input impedance at distance l from load: Z_in = Z₀ × (Zⁿ + jZ₀ tan(βl)) / (Z₀ + jZⁿ tan(βl))

Quarter-wave transformer — used to match two different impedances:

At l = λ/4: Z_in = Z₀² / Zⁿ To match Z_source to Z_load, insert a λ/4 section with: Z₀ = √(Z_source × Z_load)
Impedance matching is critical in all RF systems. A mismatch means reflected power returns to the transmitter — wasted energy and potentially damaging to amplifiers.

Module Topic 6: Antennas Q3b · Q3c — Direct Exam Questions

Antenna Parameters

Definitions
Radiation resistance R_rad: P_rad = ½ I₀² R_rad power radiated by antenna with peak current I₀ Directivity D: D(θ,φ) = U(θ,φ) / U_avg radiation intensity vs. average Gain G = η_rad × D η_rad = P_rad/P_input = radiation efficiency Effective aperture: A_eff = Gλ²/(4π)

Short (Hertzian) Dipole

Q3b
Valid when: L « λ Radiation resistance: R_rad = 80π² (L/λ)² [Ω] For Q3b: f = 200 MHz, L = 2 cm λ = c/f = 3×10&sup8; / 2×10&sup8; = 1.5 m L/λ = 0.02 / 1.5 = 0.0133 « 1 ✓ assumption valid R_rad = 80π² × (0.0133)² ≈ 0.140 Ω P_rad = ½ I₀² R_rad I₀ = √(2 × P_rad / R_rad) = √(40/0.140) ≈ 16.9 A Pattern: U ∝ sin²θ Directivity: D = 1.5
The question says "verify assumptions" — explicitly show L/λ = 0.0133 « 1. This is worth marks. Don't skip it.

Half-Wave Dipole

Q3c
Length: L = λ/2 For f = 200 MHz: λ = c/f = 1.5 m → L = 0.75 m Properties: R_rad ≈ 73 Ω radiation resistance D ≈ 1.64 slightly more directive than short dipole Z_in ≈ 73 + j42.5 Ω slightly inductive — shorten slightly for resonance
Radiation pattern sketch: vertical antenna line, two bulging lobes pointing left and right (broadside). Label: maximum at θ = 90°, nulls at θ = 0° and 180° (along axis). Nearly identical to short dipole but slightly narrower.

Friis Transmission Equation

Link Budget
P_r = P_t × G_t × G_r × (λ/4πR)² P_t = transmitted power [W] G_t, G_r = gains of transmit/receive antennas R = link distance [m] (λ/4πR)² = free-space path loss factor
Friis equation is the basis of all wireless link budget calculations — WiFi, mobile, radar, satellite. It tells you whether a received signal is detectable.

Module Topic 7: EMC & Safety Definitions Likely

Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC)

Concepts

EMC is the ability of equipment to work correctly in its electromagnetic environment without causing or suffering interference.

Three elements of any EMI problem: 1. Source — emits unwanted EM energy 2. Path — coupling mechanism (radiated, conducted, near-field) 3. Receptor — device affected EMC fixes target one or more elements: • Reduce source emission (filtering, layout) • Block coupling path (shielding, separation, twisted pairs) • Harden the receptor (filtering, grounding)

Shielding Effectiveness (SE):

SE = 20 log₁₀(E_incident / E_transmitted) [dB] SE = R + A + M (reflection + absorption + multiple reflections) Absorption loss in shield of thickness t: A = 8.686 × t/δs [dB] δs = skin depth in shield material Higher σ or higher f → smaller δs → more absorption → better shielding

Human Exposure & SAR

Safety Standards
Specific Absorption Rate (SAR): SAR = σ|E|² / ρ [W/kg] σ = tissue conductivity [S/m] ρ = tissue mass density [kg/m³] E = electric field inside tissue [V/m] Safety limits (ICNIRP): General public: SAR < 2 W/kg (averaged over 10 g, head/trunk) Occupational: SAR < 10 W/kg Mobile phones: < 2 W/kg (EU) or < 1.6 W/kg (US/FCC)
SAR links directly to the lossy media topic — the Ohmic power dissipated per unit volume is ½σ|E|². SAR is this divided by mass density.

Frequency bands and biological effects:

ELF (50/60 Hz): Power lines — induced currents in body RF (kHz–GHz): Mobile/WiFi — tissue heating (SAR) Microwave (1–100 GHz): Shallow penetration, surface heating Ionising (UV, X-ray): Photon energy breaks chemical bonds → DNA damage
Non-ionising RF/microwave radiation heats tissue but does NOT cause ionisation at normal exposure levels. Below ICNIRP limits, no established health effects exist. X-rays are a distinct ionising hazard.
