Cartesian Cylindrical Spherical

Cartesian

(x, y, z) · ê_x, ê_y, ê_z
Line Element dl
dl = dx ê_x + dy ê_y + dz ê_z
Along x-axis only: dl = dx ê_x Along y-axis only: dl = dy ê_y Along z-axis only: dl = dz ê_z
Surface Element dS
xy-plane: dS = dx dy ê_z xz-plane: dS = dx dz ê_y yz-plane: dS = dy dz ê_x
Volume Element dV
dV = dx dy dz

Cylindrical

(ρ, φ, z) · ê_ρ, ê_φ, ê_z
Line Element dl
dl = dρ ê_ρ + ρ dφ ê_φ + dz ê_z
Radial only: dl = dρ ê_ρ Around circle: dl = ρ dφ ê_φ Along axis: dl = dz ê_z
Surface Element dS
Side of cylinder: dS = ρ dφ dz ê_ρ Top/bottom disk: dS = ρ dρ dφ ê_z Wedge face: dS = dρ dz ê_φ
Volume Element dV
dV = ρ dρ dφ dz
The ρ factor comes from the arc length ρ dφ in the φ direction

Spherical

(r, θ, φ) · ê_r, ê_θ, ê_φ
Line Element dl
dl = dr ê_r + r dθ ê_θ + r sinθ dφ ê_φ
Radial only: dl = dr ê_r Along θ: dl = r dθ ê_θ Along φ: dl = r sinθ dφ ê_φ
Surface Element dS
Sphere surface (const r): dS = r² sinθ dθ dφ ê_r Cone surface (const θ): dS = r sinθ dr dφ ê_θ Wedge (const φ): dS = r dr dθ ê_φ
Volume Element dV
dV = r² sinθ dr dθ dφ
Two scale factors: r² from the radial geometry, sinθ from the latitude shrinking near the poles
Cartesian — use when Flat plates, rectangular boxes, fields given as V(x,y,z), any problem with flat parallel boundaries. Q2a (square plate) is Cartesian.
Cylindrical — use when Coaxial cables, cylinders, wires, anything with a long axis of symmetry. Common in transmission line and waveguide problems.
Spherical — use when Point charges, spherical shells, antennas (radiation patterns). V = Q/(4πεr) and E = E(r) are spherical by nature. Q2b is spherical.

Setting Up Integrals

Line Integral — ∫ F · dl

Used for: voltage from E-field, work done moving a charge

V_BA = −∫(A→B) E · dl Steps: 1. Pick a path from A to B 2. Write dl for that path/direction 3. Dot E · dl (only the matching component survives) 4. Set limits from start to end point

Example — radial path in spherical:

E = E_r(r) ê_r, dl = dr ê_r E · dl = E_r dr V_BA = −∫[r_A to r_B] E_r dr

Surface Integral — ∬ F · dS

Used for: Gauss's Law (total flux), total charge on a surface

Φ = ∬ D · dS = Q_enclosed Steps: 1. Identify the surface geometry 2. Write dS with correct ê_n (outward) 3. Dot F · dS 4. Set limits over the surface area

Example — flat plate in xy-plane (Q2a):

dS = dx' dy' ê_z Limits: x' from −L/2 to L/2 y' from −L/2 to L/2 E = ∫∫ (ρs/4πε₀) × R/|R|³ dx'dy'

Volume Integral — ∭ f dV

Used for: total charge from charge density ρᵥ

Q_total = ∭ ρᵥ dV Cartesian: ∫∫∫ ρᵥ dx dy dz Cylindrical: ∫∫∫ ρᵥ ρ dρ dφ dz Spherical: ∫∫∫ ρᵥ r² sinθ dr dθ dφ

Example — uniform sphere of charge density ρᵥ, radius R:

Q = ∫₀ᴿ ∫₀^π ∫₀^2π ρᵥ r² sinθ dφ dθ dr = ρᵥ × (4/3)πR³

Closed Surface — ∯ D · dS

Used for: Gauss's Law to find E from symmetric charge distributions

∯ D · dS = Q_enclosed Strategy — choose a Gaussian surface where E is constant and parallel to dS: Sphere → use spherical surface Cylinder → use cylindrical surface Plane → use pillbox (flat box)

Point charge — spherical Gaussian surface radius r:

D × 4πr² = Q E = Q/(4πεr²) ê_r ← Coulomb's Law

Which Coordinate System to Use

Use Cartesian when…

  • Problem has a rectangular plate or box
  • V is given as V(x, y, z)
  • Fields are uniform or vary with x/y/z
  • Boundaries are flat and parallel
  • Taking a gradient or curl of a Cartesian field

Use Cylindrical when…

  • Problem has a wire, cable, or rod
  • Coaxial cable geometry (inner + outer radii)
  • Field has rotational symmetry around z-axis
  • Waveguide cross-sections
  • V or E depends on ρ only (E = E(ρ) ê_ρ)

Use Spherical when…

  • Point charge or spherical shell
  • V = Q/(4πεr) — always spherical
  • Antenna radiation patterns (E depends on r, θ)
  • Field depends only on distance from a point
  • Gauss's Law with a spherical surface
